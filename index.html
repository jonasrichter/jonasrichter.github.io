I am a theoretical physicist working as a Postdoctoral Research Fellow at the Department of Physics and Astronomy at University College London in the group of Arijeet Pal. My research interests lie in the field of condensed matter physics and, in particular, I am interested in the dynamics of quantum many-body systems. I obtained my PhD from the University of Osnabrück under the supervision of Robin Steinigeweg. Prior to that, I studied physics at the Technical University of Braunschweig, where I attained my bachelor's and my master's degree.

Research interests


Below, I highlight some of my current research interests. For a more complete picture, you can also have a look at my list of publications.

Eigenstate thermalization hypothesis and random matrix theory

The eigenstate thermalization hypothesis (ETH) assumes a certain matrix structure of physical operators in the eigenbasis of generic Hamiltonians. Here, we have studied the question to what extent local operators can be represented as random matrices and, in particular, to what extent matrix elements can be considered as uncorrelated. As a main result, we show that the eigenvalue distribution of band submatrices at a fixed energy density is a sensitive probe of the correlations between matrix elements. We find that, on the scales where the matrix elements are in a good agreement with all standard indicators of the ETH, the eigenvalue distribution still exhibits clear signatures of the original operator, implying correlations between matrix elements. Moreover, we demonstrate that at much smaller energy scales, the eigenvalue distribution approximately assumes the universal semicircle shape, indicating transition to the random-matrix behavior, and in particular that matrix elements become uncorrelated.
  • Phys. Rev. E 102, 042127 (2020)

Far-from-equilibrium dynamics and thermalization

While the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis (ETH) provides an explanation for the occurrence of thermalization in isolated quantum systems, much less is known about the full time-dependent relaxation process prior to equilibration. We have studied this route to equilibrium for a realistic class of initial states which can be prepared close to as well as far away from equilibrium. Here, a controlled point of reference is provided by linear response theory (LRT), which describes the dynamics in situations close to equilibrium. We have shown that, if the off-diagonal part of the ETH applies, then the relaxation process can become independent of the initial state. In this case, the dynamics is generated by the relaxation function from LRT, even in far-from-equilibrium situations.

  • Phys. Rev. E 99, 012114 (2019)
  • Phys. Rev. E 99, 050104(R) (2019)

For a double-quench protocol where an external force is suddenly switched on an off, we have found that the relaxation of ETH-violating systems can exhibit an intriguing dependence on the strength of the external force. For small forces within the validity regime of LRT, thermalization occurs despite the ETH being violated. In contrast, for stronger forces beyond linear response, observables equilibrate towards nonthermal values which depend on the previous nonequilibrium protocol.

  • Phys. Rev. E 100, 032124 (2019)

Many-body localization

Many-body localization (MBL) can occur in strongly disordered quantum systems and generalizes the phenomenon of Anderson localization to the presence of interactions. In the context of transport, we have discussed the influence of disorder for large particle-particle interactions, where normal diffusion is known to occur in the disorder-free case. Our results suggest that normal diffusion is stable against weak disorder, while they are consistent with anomalous diffusion for stronger disorder below the localization transition.

  • Phys. Rev. B 98, 134302 (2018)

While many works have explored MBL in disordered spin-1/2 systems, much less is known about the effect of disorder for larger spin quantum numbers S>1/2. Since MBL is believed to be a pure quantum phenomenon, it is an open question to what extent localization can occur upon increasing S, where the quantum spins become more and more akin to their classical counterparts. We have shown that the putative MBL transition is shifted to considerably stronger values of disorder for increasing S. Moreover, we have introduced an effective disorder strength, which provides a mapping between the dynamics for different spin quantum numbers.

  • Phys. Rev. B 100, 144423 (2019)
  • Phys. Rev. Research 2, 013130 (2020)

Quantum transport

One strategy to analyze transport properties is provided by the framework of linear response theory, where transport coefficients are expressed in terms of time-dependent correlation functions evaluated in an equilibrium ensemble. Studying spatiotemporal correlation functions for comparatively large system sizes and long time scales, we have shown that diffusive transport occurs in various nonintegrable lattice models and manifests itself in, e.g., Gaussian density profiles, exponentially decaying density modes, and Lorentzian line shapes of the dynamical structure factor.

  • Phys. Rev. B 97, 174430 (2018)
  • Phys. Rev. B 99, 144422 (2019)
Moreover, we have demonstrated that the nonintegrable S=1 Heisenberg chain shows signatures of superdiffusive spin transport, analogous to its integrable S=1/2 counterpart, at least on the time and length scales numerically accessible.
  • Phys. Rev. B 100, 144423 (2019)
  • Phys. Rev. Research 2, 013130 (2020)

Numerical approaches to quantum many-body dynamics

Simulating the dynamics of quantum many-body systems is challenging. This is not least caused by the exponentially growing Hilbert space which limits exact diagonalization to small system sizes. Substantially larger Hilbert spaces can be treated by, e.g., the concept of dynamical quantum typicality (DQT), where static and dynamic expectation values are evaluated on the basis of pure quantum states which mimic the statistical ensemble. DQT provides an efficient means to study (i) time-dependent equilibrium correlation functions, (ii) thermodynamic quantities, and (iii) the far-from-equilibrium dynamics resulting from certain quench scenarios.
  • Z. Naturforsch. A 75, 465 (2020)
  • Z. Naturforsch. A 75, 421 (2020)
Numerical linked cluster expansions (NLCEs) provide a means to study quantum systems directly in the thermodynamic limit. Specifically, we have shown that NLCEs can be used to obtain time-dependent equilibrium correlation functions and that the range of accessible cluster sizes can be extended by combining NLCEs with DQT. Moreover, we have demonstrated that NLCEs comprising solely rectangular clusters provide a promising approach to study the real-time dynamics of two-dimensional quantum many-body systems. Due to the simple cluster geometry, the organization of the NLCE becomes straightforward and larger expansion orders can be included thanks to a memory efficient pure-state propagation. As a consequence, the NLCE can yield converged results on time scales which are competitive to other state-of-the-art numerical methods.
  • Phys. Rev. B 99, 094419 (2019)
  • Phys. Rev. E 101, 062133 (2020)
  • SciPost Phys. 9, 031 (2020)
