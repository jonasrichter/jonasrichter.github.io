Moving to California as a Marie Curie Fellow

14. November 2022

Working in academia as a postdoctoral researcher has positive and negative sides. Very often postdocs only spend a short time, say two to three years, at a given place before moving on to the next (postdoc) job at a new institution in a different city, or even in another country. Whether this frequent moving counts as positive or negative at the end of the day is probably up to personal taste, but it is for sure exciting and daunting at the same time.
For the last two years, I was living in London - the beautiful capital of the United Kingdom - where I was a postdoc doing research in quantum physics at University College London. Together with my wife, we moved to London from Germany in 2020 in the middle of the Corona pandemic. Unsurprisingly, London turned out to be an amazing city (with surprisingly better weather than often claimed), and UCL is a great university with brilliant people from whom I learned a lot.
In spring of 2022, however, a new life chapter started to announce itself - unbelievable but true, I was notified that my application for a competitive Marie Skłodowska-Curie Postdoctoral Fellowship was indeed successful. My project MaBoQuaCo, or in long form, "Quantum Many-Body Dynamics and Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum Computers: Interconnections, Near-Term Applications, and Novel Simulation Schemes" was selected to be generously funded by the European Research Commission. The project has a duration of 2.5 years and opens up the fantastic opportunity to conduct independent research at two world-leading universities - Stanford University in the US, and Leibniz Universität Hannover in Germany - where I will be kindly hosted by the groups of V. Khemani and T. J. Osborne, respectively. After two years in the UK, we eventually said goodbye to London in September 2022.
Fast forward to today, I am now working since two weeks in my new position. In the proposal that I submitted to the European Commission, I have outlined my research ideas that I intend to pursue over the course of the project. Broadly speaking, I will study various aspects of nonequilibrium quantum matter, with a particular focus on applications of current or near-term noisy quantum computers. Quantum computers harness the laws of quantum mechanics and promise to solve certain problems more efficiently than their classical counterparts. One such problem is the simulation of many-body quantum systems out of equilibrium, which is notoriously difficult for conventional - classical - computers as the computational requirements grow dramatically (that is, exponentially) with increasing the size of the quantum system.
Unfortunately, fully-fledged quantum computing is still far into the future and today's noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices are prone to errors due to the influence of the environment and imperfect control over the qubits. Building on the background that I gained during my PhD in Osnabrück and my postdoc in London, my goal for this project is to advance our understanding of the universal properties of quantum dynamics, for instance in driven-dissipative systems, in circuit models comprised of unitary gates potentially interspersed with measurements, and in many-body localized systems coupled to a thermal bath. Progress in these directions will help to unravel the inevitable interaction of NISQ devices with their environment and, in turn, will hopefully lead to more robust and scalable NISQ simulations. Similarly, the necessity to benchmark larger and larger NISQ devices may give rise to new efficient numerical simulation methods that can be used to tackle challenging questions with classical computers. I will share more details on the progress of my project in future posts in this blog.
Stanford University, and especially the Geballe Laboratory for Advanced Materials where I am currently located, is a great place to do Physics - there is free coffee (and free donuts on Mondays). Most importantly, one is surrounded by incredibly intelligent and inspiring people who are experts in cutting-edge analytical, numerical, or experimental techniques, and study questions at the forefront of current research. Working in this setting is definitely an amazing, but also humbling, experience. From my first two weeks, I further have the impression that there definitely exists a form of work-life balance (in case one is allowed to have such a thing as a postdoc): everyone works extremely hard, but one also gets rewarded by California's sunny weather, an abundance of leisure activities, and the remarkable natural beauty that the San Francisco Bay Area has to offer.
Moving to a new country, however, also entails a number of unavoidable administrative tasks that can consume a big chunk of mental capacity and can be quite detrimental to one's productivity (everyone who has moved abroad before can probably relate). Nevertheless, I am starting to explore various different research direction and am gaining more and more momentum every day. I am very much looking forward to what the next months have in store.
