Looking back on 2022

19. December 2022

Slowly but surely, the year 2022 is coming to an end. And what a year it has been. After two years of online workshops due to the Corona pandemic, I was finally able to visit an in-person conference again - the APS March Meeting in Chicago. The hybrid version was a little weird and there were definitely a couple of things that didn't work as smoothly as they should have. But it was still great to see so many physicists, meet up with some colleagues and friends, and simply visit a new place that I haven't been to before. For me, visiting the March Meeting each year (or the Spring Meeting of the German Physical Society) is a nice tradition which also serves as an important source of my motivation. The fixed date every year and the goal of presenting cool new results always gives me a little extra push to get some projects done. I am already looking forward to the March Meeting 2023, which will take place in Las Vegas - definitely very exciting!

What else (as far as physics is concerned) happened in 2022? Overall, I was quite busy with juggling multiple projects simultaneously (I guess many postdocs can relate) and trying to make progress in all of them. In March, I already knew that I was going to leave London in September to move on to my new position in the US. During the summer, I therefore ambitiously aimed to wrap up all my running projects, such that I can start my Marie-Curie project without too much "ballast". I am happy to say that I more or less managed to achieve this goal. I am also quite happy how 2022 turned out in terms of publications and, at least for me personally, I can say that I learned a lot of new stuff from the projects that I worked on. In August and September, I also attended three other in-person conferences, namely a workshop at the MPIPKS in Dresden, the German Spring Meeting in Regensburg (which was postponed to September), and a workshop taking place in the picturesque Logar Valley in Slovenia. All three were a lot of fun, but especially the last one in Slovenia turned out to be absolutely amazing in terms of location, food, and obviously physics!

Now, it's December and Christmas is around the corner. This seems like a good time for a quick review of some of the research that I did this year (or rather a review of the general field that I am working in). I will try to keep this superficial, so if you want to learn more please feel free to check out my publications and, of course, the references cited therein.
One research area that I am especially interested in is thermalization in isolated quantum many-body systems. That is, studying if and how a quantum system, initially prepared in an out-of-equilibrium state, evolves towards thermal equilibrium at long times. If the quantum system additionally possesses a global conservation law, the process of thermalization is closely connected with the transport properties of the system. Consider, for instance, a system of N particles moving in a one-dimensional lattice with discrete sites and interacting with each other. Often, one encounters problems where the overall particle number N is conserved, that is, there are no terms in the Hamiltonian that add or remove a particle from the system. Now, imagine that the system is prepared in a state with all particles in the left half, while all lattice sites in the right half are empty. Clearly, this is a far-from-equilibrium state, since in equilibrium we would expect a globally homogeneous distribution of the particles. In order for the system to reach thermal equilibrium, particles thus have to be transported from the left to the right.
These transport processes are typically rather slow compared to other relevant time scales in the system. Therefore, in order to understand the time scale of eventual thermalization, it is crucial to investigate the transport properties. For generic, non-fine-tuned systems, a naive first guess is that the particles behave diffusively as if they would undergo a random walk on average. While diffusion is arguably the default expectation is the case of classical physics, its emergence in the quantum world is actually not entirely obvious. Nevertheless, diffusive transport is routinely found in (numerical) experiments in a variety of isolated quantum many-body systems, and a series of recent influential works have put these observations on firm theoretical footing. However, despite this progress, it still remains a remarkably challenging problem to actually extract quantitative values for the corresponding diffusion constants even with state-of-the-art analytical or numerical approaches. Furthermore, while models with diffusion might be the rule, there are also interesting exceptions that show anomalous types of transport such as fast superdiffusion or slower subdiffusion.
While the vast majority of quantum many-body systems eventually reaches thermal equilibrium under their own unitary dynamics, some selected types of systems are believed to evade this thermal fate. A popular example are strongly disordered models undergoing a many-body localization transition. These models behave as insulators (that is, no transport) even at finite temperatures. Many-body localization has been an exceptionally active research area in the last two decades but, rather recently, there have been quite a few new developments. Regions in parameter space that were originally believed to be strongly localized are now often interpreted as thermalizing, albeit on astronomically long time scales. This already summarizes the main problem when studying MBL. Essentially, in order to unambiguously decide if a quantum system indeed localizes or, more formally, if many-body localization is indeed a stable phase of matter, one would have to study systems of infinite size and up to infinite times. In practice, this is simply impossible both for experiments as well as for numerics. Nevertheless, we have tried to make some progress by coming up with helpful numerical tricks and by looking at models that have received comparatively less attention so far.
Studying the dynamics of quantum systems is generally quite challenging. On the one hand, analytical solutions are rather rare and often apply only to certain fine-tuned models. On the other hand, numerical approaches, while certainly extremely useful, are pushed to their limits due to the exponential complexity of many-body quantum systems. Motivated by these challenges, and thanks to helpful influences from the field of quantum information, a new framework has become quite popular in recent years: random quantum circuits. These circuits are designed as minimally structured models to describe the universal aspects of quantum dynamics. They exhibit some key properties, such as locality, unitarity, and potentially the presence of conservation laws, but are otherwise as unstructured as possible. In some cases, this randomness has the advantage that one can actually obtain analytical insights that would have been inaccessible otherwise. Moreover, certain classes of circuits can be simulated efficiently even on classical computers for huge system sizes. This applies in particular to circuits composed of so-called Clifford gates, which are very well-known in the quantum-information community but only recently got attention of physicists working on questions in condensed-matter or quantum many-body physics. It is definitely a lot of fun to suddenly be able to simulate systems with thousands of lattice sites, whereas before one was already happy to handle a few dozens of particles. Understanding which phenomena these circuits can or cannot capture is one area I am currently interested in.

So, what's next? I have several things on my list that I want to learn more about and that I plan to work on. As usual, 2023 promises to be a busy year. Now, in the final days before the Christmas break, I will try to start as many simulations as possible and let them run on big computer clusters. Hopefully, these simulations will produce some nice data for the projects that I am currently working on. Letting the computers do the hard work while I get some days off always feels very satisfying!

Finally, I share two pictures with you that I took this year. The first one is from the March Meeting in Chicago and shows the bright green river thanks to St. Patrick's Day. The other one shows the Logar Valley, Slovenia, with the beautiful view that we got to enjoy from the conference hotel for one week.
