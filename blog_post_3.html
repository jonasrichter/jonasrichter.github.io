California, Casino, and Clifford circuits

20. March 2023

It's been a while since my last blog entry. The beginning of the year has been busy, but now I finally managed to sit down and write some paragraphs about what has happened in the last couple weeks and, of course, about some physics stuff that I am currently interested in.

Weather-wise, the Californian winter has been surprisingly unstable. While I am definitely happy to avoid the cold and snowy European version (as well as all the other winter storms that happened elsewhere in the US), I learned that the sun is not always shining in California. It has been raining quite heavily over the last couple months. Moreover, the rain is usually accompanied by strong winds and, given the sometimes interesting American infrastructure, I am writing these lines during the second power outage we had in two months. Having lived in Germany for 27 years, I cannot remember a single blackout. Now, during 2.5 years in the UK and the US, this is already the fourth time that I have to rely on candlelight in the evening.

On another note, the APS March Meeting took place last week in Las Vegas. I have been looking forward to this event for quite some time. Overall, it was a lot of fun! I met some colleagues and friends from Europe, listened to some interesting talks, and went on a day trip to the amazing Valley of Fire. In hindsight, however, I must admit that this year's March Meeting will not make my all-time favorite-conference list. The convention center was simply way too small such that many sessions ended up overcrowded which made it hard to enjoy the talks. Moreover, food options in Las Vegas were in my opinion not too great and, even though I am used to Silicon-Valley prices, everything was remarkably expensive.

At the March Meeting, I presented a talk about my recent publication on transport and entanglement growth in long-range random Clifford circuits that I worked on in collaboration with Oliver Lunt (Kings College London) and Arijeet Pal (University College London). I was positively surprised that a lot of people visited the session despite being scheduled on Friday morning. Also, at least from what I can tell from my biased point of view, I think that my talk went quite well and I was happy to get many good questions from the audience (the speaker after me didn't show up which gave me some bonus time).

Let me try to explain why I am currently quite excited about working on Clifford circuits (though there probably exist already much better resources that are more visual and more pedagogical). First of all, when you are used to the usual limitations of numerical simulations in quantum dynamics, working with Clifford circuits is extremely enjoyable! In particular, in contrast to fully generic quantum evolution, Clifford circuits can be efficiently simulated on classical computers such that huge system sizes and long time scales can be accessed. This can be extremely useful as some effects can only be clearly analyzed if the systems are large enough.

Usually, in order to describe the state of a many-body quantum system, the computer's memory has to store a complex vector, with the number of entries growing exponentially with the size of the system. For example, given a system of L qubits (i.e., L coupled two-level units), one needs to store (and manipulate!) 2L complex numbers. Therefore, despite the increasing power of computers, relatively small quantum systems somewhere between L = 20 to L = 45 can push even modern supercomputers to their limits (depending on some details of the system and what exactly one wants to compute).

In contrast, in the case of Clifford circuits, systems with thousands of qubits can be simulated even on a laptop. This is possible if one only considers so-called stabilizer states. These stabilizer states have the property that they can be uniquely specified by a set of Pauli-strings (i.e., operators composed of Pauli matrices at different sites). Using this fact, stabilizer states can be compactly represented in the form of a binary matrix that only grows linearly in the number of qubits. Importantly, acting on a stabilizer state with a unitary operation that belongs to the Clifford group will yield another stabilizer state. Therefore, memory and time requirements to simulate circuits that consist entirely of Clifford elements don't exhibit the exponential explosion that occurs for other classes of quantum dynamics.

Is the quantum dynamics studied in Clifford circuits generic (i.e., does it reflect the vast majority of non-fine-tuned quantum systems)? The answer is clearly no. As explained above, not all quantum states can be generated using the Clifford framework. Nevertheless, Clifford circuits can still prove useful as they mimic certain properties of more generic quantum evolutions, e.g., they generate extensive amounts of entanglement.

In our paper, we use Clifford circuits to study the interplay of transport and entanglement growth in quantum systems. Specifically, earlier work has demonstrated that in quantum systems with diffusive transport, this diffusive transport leaves a fingerprint in the dynamics of entanglement which in turn grows slower than traditionally expected. In our paper, we consider long-range systems where the transport behavior can be tuned to different regimes by varying the power law that controls the range of interactions in the circuit. In a nutshell, we find that for all these regimes, the entanglement growth mimics the transport behavior and is controlled by the same dynamical exponent. Another nice thing about Clifford circuits is that due to their comparatively simple structure, it is possible to come up with easy-to-understand pictures to explain our results.

Recently, we also studied other types of Clifford circuits - namely Floquet circuits where the spatially random gates occur in a time-periodic fashion. As a main result, we show that these circuits exhibit a strong form a localization in one dimension, but are delocalized in two dimensions. Localization is quite an active research area in quantum many-body physics and I personally find it quite interesting to shed light on this question using Clifford dynamics. Generally, I believe it is an intriguing direction to better understand what types of exotic nonequilibrium quantum phenomena can be modeled using Clifford circuits. I hope to study this question in more detail in the future.
